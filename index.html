White Paper: Modeling Pension Costs

By: Josh McGee and Michelle H. Welch

In this brief we present our method for projecting pension costs. The calculations presented below are based on the Entry Age Normal cost method. We assume that each year, the plan sponsor pays the full Annual Required Contribution (ARC), which includes the cost of benefits earned in that year, normal cost, and amortization on any overfunding or underfunding. We assume that all plan assumptions hold true and importantly that the plan achieves its assumed investment rate of return. Therefore, there is no future underfunding in our model, that is, we assume that the plan sponsor‚Äôs funding policy matches true plan cost for all future years.

Normal Cost

Normal cost is generally defined as the contribution amount necessary to cover retirement benefits earned in any given year under the plan sponsor‚Äôs chosen funding policy. 1 While the actuarial profession uses a number of methods to calculate normal cost, Entry Age Normal (EAN), the method we use below, is the most prevalent and is the method specified by the new GASB accounting standards.2 Under the EAN method, normal cost is calculated as the constant percentage of wages that if contributed across a worker‚Äôs career, would fully cover the benefit payments earned by the worker. The calculation of normal cost takes into account the worker separation distribution, mortality, salary growth, and anticipated earnings on contributions.

Normal cost is calculated by dividing the expected value of future retirement benefits at age of workforce entry by the expected cumulative wages at entry. To calculate the plan sponsor‚Äôs share of retirement benefits cost, we simply net cumulative worker contributions out of earned pension benefits at each point across a worker‚Äôs career.
